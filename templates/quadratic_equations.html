What is a quadratic equation?

A quadratic equation is an equation whose highest power of the variable is \(2\). In standard form, all terms are moved to one side and the other side is \(0\).

\(ax^2+bx+c=0,\quad a\ne0\)
Coefficient \(a\)

Controls whether the parabola opens upward or downward and how wide it is.

Coefficient \(b\)

Helps determine the axis of symmetry and shifts the vertex.

Constant \(c\)

The \(y\)-intercept: the point where the graph crosses the \(y\)-axis.

The solutions of a quadratic equation are the \(x\)-values where the parabola meets, touches, or fails to meet the \(x\)-axis.

Why can a quadratic have \(2\), \(1\), or \(0\) real answers?

A quadratic equation is solved by finding where the parabola has height \(0\). In graph language, this means we look for intersections with the \(x\)-axis.

\(D=b^2-4ac\)
\(D>0\)

Two distinct real solutions

The parabola crosses the \(x\)-axis twice.

\(D=0\)

One repeated real solution

The parabola just touches the \(x\)-axis.

\(D<0\)

No real solutions

The parabola does not meet the \(x\)-axis.

The discriminant does not give the roots directly. It first tells you how many real roots to expect. Then you solve using factoring, square roots, or the quadratic formula.

Which method should I use?

Factoring

Use it when the quadratic expression splits nicely into two brackets.

\((x-2)(x-3)=0\)
Square root method

Use it when there is no \(x\)-term or when a squared expression is isolated.

\(x^2=25\)
Quadratic formula

Use it when factoring is not obvious. This method always works.

\(x=\dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{D}}{2a}\)

Types of quadratic equations

A quadratic equation may be complete or incomplete. The missing term changes the easiest solving method.

Case 1 — No \(bx\) term

If the equation looks like \(ax^2+c=0\), isolate \(x^2\), then take both positive and negative square roots.

\(x^2-16=0\Rightarrow x^2=16\Rightarrow x=\pm4\)
Case 2 — No constant term

If the equation looks like \(ax^2+bx=0\), factor out \(x\). One solution will usually be \(x=0\).

\(2x^2-6x=0\Rightarrow 2x(x-3)=0\Rightarrow x=0,3\)
Case 3 — Complete quadratic

If all three terms are present, try factoring first. If it does not factor easily, use the formula.

\(ax^2+bx+c=0\)

Example 1 — two real solutions

Solve:

\(x^2-5x+6=0\)
First check the discriminant: \(D=(-5)^2-4(1)(6)=25-24=1\). Since \(D>0\), expect two real solutions.
Find two numbers that multiply to \(6\) and add to \(-5\): \(-2\) and \(-3\).
Factor: \(x^2-5x+6=(x-2)(x-3)\).
Set each factor to zero: \(x-2=0\) or \(x-3=0\).

Answer:

\(x=2,\quad x=3\)

Graph meaning: the parabola crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(2\) and \(3\).

Example 2 — one repeated solution

Solve:

\(x^2-6x+9=0\)
Compute the discriminant: \(D=(-6)^2-4(1)(9)=36-36=0\).
Since \(D=0\), there is one repeated solution. The parabola touches the \(x\)-axis only once.
Factor: \(x^2-6x+9=(x-3)^2\).
Solve: \((x-3)^2=0\Rightarrow x=3\).

Answer:

\(x=3\)

Example 3 — no real solution

Solve:

\(x^2+4=0\)
Move \(4\) to the other side: \(x^2=-4\).
A square of a real number cannot be negative, so there is no real solution.
Discriminant check: \(D=0^2-4(1)(4)=-16<0\), so the graph does not cross the \(x\)-axis.

Answer:

\(\varnothing\)

Example 4 — quadratic formula, step by step

Solve:

\(2x^2+3x-2=0\)
Identify the coefficients: \(a=2\), \(b=3\), \(c=-2\).
Find the discriminant: \(D=3^2-4(2)(-2)=9+16=25\).
Substitute into the formula: \(x=\dfrac{-3\pm\sqrt{25}}{2\cdot2}\).
Simplify: \(x=\dfrac{-3\pm5}{4}\).
Split into two answers: \(x=\dfrac{-3+5}{4}=\dfrac12\), and \(x=\dfrac{-3-5}{4}=-2\).

Answer:

\(x=\dfrac12,\quad x=-2\)

Vertex and axis of symmetry

The vertex is the turning point of the parabola. It is the lowest point if \(a>0\) and the highest point if \(a<0\). The vertical line through the vertex is called the axis of symmetry.

\(x_{\text{vertex}}=-\dfrac{b}{2a}\)
Example

For \(y=x^2-4x+3\), we have \(a=1\) and \(b=-4\), so \(x_{\text{vertex}}=-\dfrac{-4}{2(1)}=2\). Then \(y(2)=4-8+3=-1\), so the vertex is \((2,-1)\).

Common mistakes

Forgetting \(\pm\)

When solving \(x^2=25\), the answer is not only \(5\). It is \(x=\pm5\).

Using the formula with wrong signs

If \(b=-6\), then \(-b=6\). Keep parentheses when substituting.

Thinking \(D<0\) means no solution at all

It means no real solution. In advanced math, there are complex solutions.

Forgetting to set the equation equal to \(0\)

Factoring works after everything is moved to one side: \(ax^2+bx+c=0\).
