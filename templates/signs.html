Working with Signs — Detailed Guide

This page explains how positive (+) and negative (−) signs behave in different arithmetic operations. Each section provides a clear rule and one simple example for illustration.

1) Addition of Signed Numbers

When adding numbers, always look at their signs first. If both numbers have the same sign, add their magnitudes and keep that sign. If they have different signs, subtract the smaller magnitude from the larger one, and keep the sign of the larger number.

Example: -5 + 2 = -3

2) Subtraction of Signed Numbers

Subtraction is really just addition of the opposite. Rewriting every subtraction as addition makes it easier: a - b = a + (-b). This way, the same rules of addition apply. Subtracting a negative means you are actually adding a positive.

Example: 4 - (-7) = 11

3) Multiplication and Division

Signs in multiplication and division follow a simple flip rule. If there is an even number of negative signs, the result is positive. If there is an odd number of negatives, the result is negative. You only multiply or divide the magnitudes, then decide the sign at the end.

Example: (-2) * 5 * -3 = 30

4) Exponents

When raising a negative base to a power, the parity (even or odd) of the exponent matters. An even exponent cancels out the negative sign, producing a positive result. An odd exponent keeps the negative sign. Parentheses are important: without them, the minus sign may only apply after the exponent is calculated.

Example: (-3)^3 = -27

5) Order of Operations

To avoid mistakes with signs, always follow the order of operations: Parentheses → Exponents → Multiplication/Division → Addition/Subtraction. Handling multiplication first is especially important when negatives are involved, as it changes how the final sign appears.

Example: 2 - 3 * (-2) = 8
This guide summarizes the key principles of working with signs. Focus on the rules, then check each step carefully when solving problems to avoid small sign mistakes.
