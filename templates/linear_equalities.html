What is a linear equation?

A linear equation is an equation in which the unknown variable appears only to the first power. In one variable, it can usually be rewritten in the form \(ax+b=c\), where \(a\neq0\). Solving the equation means finding the value of the variable that makes the equality true.

\(ax+b=c,\qquad a\neq0\)
Variable

The unknown value, usually written as \(x\). The goal is to find its value.

Coefficient

The number multiplying the variable. In \(7x-3=18\), the coefficient of \(x\) is \(7\).

Constant term

A number without a variable. In \(7x-3=18\), the constant term on the left is \(-3\).

Solution

A value that makes the equation true after substitution.

The most important idea is balance: the left side and right side must remain equal after every operation.

Interactive graph maker

A linear equation in two variables has the form \(y=mx+b\). Its graph is a straight line. The number \(m\) controls the slope, and \(b\) controls where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis.

Move the sliders. Watch how the slope, \(y\)-intercept, and \(x\)-intercept change.

\(y = mx + b\)
Slope \(m\)

If \(m>0\), the line rises from left to right. If \(m<0\), the line falls. If \(m=0\), the line is horizontal.

Intercept \(b\)

The value \(b\) is the point where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis: \((0,b)\).

The balance principle

An equation is like a balanced scale. If two expressions are equal, then adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing both sides by the same valid number keeps them equal.

\(A=B\quad\Longrightarrow\quad A+k=B+k\)
\(A=B\quad\Longrightarrow\quad A-k=B-k\)
\(A=B\quad\Longrightarrow\quad kA=kB\)
\(A=B\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \dfrac{A}{k}=\dfrac{B}{k},\quad k\neq0\)
Never divide by \(0\). Division by zero is undefined, so it cannot be used as a solving step.

Reliable solving process

Almost every one-variable linear equation can be solved with the same logical process. Do not memorize random tricks; follow the structure.

Clear parentheses using the distributive property.
Clear fractions if needed by multiplying by the least common denominator.
Combine like terms on each side.
Move all variable terms to one side and constants to the other side.
Divide by the coefficient of the variable.
Check the solution by substituting it into the original equation.
A good solution is not just the final answer. It shows why each step keeps the equation equivalent.

Moving terms across the equals sign

When a term is moved from one side of the equals sign to the other, its operation changes to the inverse operation. This is not magic; it is a shorter way of doing the same operation to both sides.

+a→-a

Addition becomes subtraction

\(x+5=12\Rightarrow x=12-5=7\)
-a→+a

Subtraction becomes addition

\(x-3=8\Rightarrow x=8+3=11\)
\(\times a\)→\(\div a\)

Multiplication becomes division

\(3x=15\Rightarrow x=15\div3=5\)
\(\div a\)→\(\times a\)

Division becomes multiplication

\(\dfrac{x}{4}=6\Rightarrow x=6\cdot4=24\)
For teaching beginners, it is safer to say “apply the inverse operation to both sides.” After they understand balance, “move across and change the sign” becomes a useful shortcut.

Main types of linear equations

TypeTypical formStrategy
Simple equation\(ax+b=c\)Move the constant, then divide by the coefficient.
Variables on both sides\(ax+b=cx+d\)Move variable terms to one side and constants to the other.
Parentheses\(a(x+b)=c\)Distribute first, then solve normally.
Fractions\(\dfrac{x}{a}+b=c\)Multiply by the denominator or LCD to remove fractions.
Special cases\(0x=k\) or \(0x=0\)Decide whether there is no solution or infinitely many solutions.

Detailed examples

Example 1Two-step equation

Solve:

\(2x+5=17\)
Subtract \(5\) from both sides: \(2x=12\).
Divide both sides by \(2\): \(x=6\).
Check: \(2(6)+5=17\), so the answer is correct.

\(x=6\)

Example 2Variables on both sides

Solve:

\(3x-4=x+10\)
Subtract \(x\) from both sides: \(2x-4=10\).
Add \(4\) to both sides: \(2x=14\).
Divide by \(2\): \(x=7\).

\(x=7\)

Example 3Equation with parentheses

Solve:

\(3(x+2)=x+14\)
Open the parentheses: \(3x+6=x+14\).
Subtract \(x\) from both sides: \(2x+6=14\).
Subtract \(6\): \(2x=8\).
Divide by \(2\): \(x=4\).

\(x=4\)

Example 4Equation with fractions

Solve:

\(\dfrac{x}{3}+5=11\)
Subtract \(5\) from both sides: \(\dfrac{x}{3}=6\).
Multiply both sides by \(3\): \(x=18\).
Check: \(\dfrac{18}{3}+5=6+5=11\).

\(x=18\)

Example 5Fractions on both sides

Solve:

\(\dfrac{x}{2}+\dfrac{x}{3}=10\)
The least common denominator of \(2\) and \(3\) is \(6\).
Multiply every term by \(6\): \(3x+2x=60\).
Combine like terms: \(5x=60\).
Divide by \(5\): \(x=12\).

\(x=12\)

Example 6Negative coefficient

Solve:

\(-4x+7=19\)
Subtract \(7\): \(-4x=12\).
Divide by \(-4\): \(x=-3\).
A negative answer is normal. It means the solution is left of \(0\) on the number line.

\(x=-3\)

Special cases: one, none, or infinitely many solutions

Most beginner examples have one solution, but linear equations can also have no solution or infinitely many solutions.

One solution

After simplifying, the equation becomes \(x=a\).

\(2x+1=7\Rightarrow x=3\)
No solution

The variable disappears and the remaining statement is false.

\(2x+5=2x+9\Rightarrow 5=9\)
Infinitely many solutions

The variable disappears and the remaining statement is true.

\(3(x+2)=3x+6\Rightarrow 6=6\)
Zero coefficient form

If the equation becomes \(0x=0\), every number works. If it becomes \(0x=k\) with \(k\neq0\), no number works.

Word problem model

In word problems, first define the unknown, then translate the sentence into an equation.

Word caseAge-style problem

A number increased by \(8\) is equal to \(23\). Find the number.

Let the number be \(x\).
“Increased by \(8\)” means \(x+8\).
The equation is \(x+8=23\).
Subtract \(8\): \(x=15\).

\(x=15\)

Common mistakes

Changing only one side

If you subtract \(5\) from the left side, you must subtract \(5\) from the right side too.

Forgetting to distribute

\(3(x+2)\) becomes \(3x+6\), not \(3x+2\).

Losing the negative sign

When dividing by a negative coefficient, the answer may become negative.

Not checking the answer

Substitution catches many arithmetic mistakes, especially in equations with fractions.

A clean linear-equation solution should be readable line by line: each new line must follow logically from the previous one.

Summary

  • A linear equation has the variable to the first power.
  • Solving means finding the value that makes the equality true.
  • The equation must stay balanced after every operation.
  • Use inverse operations to isolate the variable.
  • Always check the final answer in the original equation.
