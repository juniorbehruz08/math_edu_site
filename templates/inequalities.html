Inequalities — Complete Introduction

An inequality is a mathematical statement that compares two expressions. Instead of saying two sides are exactly equal, it says one side is greater, smaller, or possibly equal to the other side.

\(< \quad > \quad \le \quad \ge\)

Equations usually give one exact answer. Inequalities often give a whole range of answers.

Main idea: An inequality describes a set of values, not just one value.

1Inequality Symbols

Each inequality symbol tells us how two expressions are related.

Symbol Meaning Boundary
\(a<b\) \(a\) is less than \(b\). Not included
\(a>b\) \(a\) is greater than \(b\). Not included
\(a\le b\) \(a\) is less than or equal to \(b\). Included
\(a\ge b\) \(a\) is greater than or equal to \(b\). Included

2Equation vs Inequality

An equation asks for exact equality. An inequality asks which values make a comparison true.

Equation

Uses \(=\). Usually gives an exact value.

\(x+2=5 \Rightarrow x=3\)
Inequality

Uses \(<, >, \le, \ge\). Often gives a range.

\(x+2>5 \Rightarrow x>3\)

3Solving Linear Inequalities

Linear inequalities are solved almost like linear equations. The key difference appears when you multiply or divide by a negative number.

Important rule: When you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, reverse the inequality sign.

Example 1: Positive coefficient

\(3x+2>11\)
Subtract \(2\) from both sides: \(3x>9\).
Divide by \(3\). The sign stays the same: \(x>3\).
✓Final Answer: \(x>3\)

Example 2: Negative coefficient

\(-2x+4\le10\)
Subtract \(4\) from both sides: \(-2x\le6\).
Divide by \(-2\), so the sign flips: \(x\ge-3\).
✓Final Answer: \(x\ge-3\)

4Double Inequalities

A double inequality has three parts. Whatever operation you do, you must do it to all three parts.

\(2<x+1\le7\)
Subtract \(1\) from all three parts.
The result is \(1<x\le6\).
✓Final Answer: \(1<x\le6\)

6Linear Inequalities with Two Variables

A two-variable linear inequality divides the coordinate plane into two regions. The boundary line separates the solution side from the non-solution side.

Strict inequality

\(<\) or \(>\) uses a dashed boundary line.

Inclusive inequality

\(\le\) or \(\ge\) uses a solid boundary line.

\(y>1.0x+0.0\)

The shaded region shows all points \((x,y)\) that satisfy the inequality.

7Quadratic Inequalities

A quadratic inequality involves a parabola. To solve it, find where the expression is positive, negative, or equal to zero.

\(x^2-5x+6>0\)
Factor: \((x-2)(x-3)\).
Roots are \(x=2\) and \(x=3\).
The expression is positive outside the roots.
✓Final Answer: \(x<2\) or \(x>3\)
Remember: Roots split the number line into intervals. Test each interval or use the graph.

9Common Mistakes

1. Forgetting to flip the sign

When multiplying or dividing by a negative number, the inequality sign must reverse.

2. Including the boundary incorrectly

Strict signs \(<\) and \(>\) exclude the boundary. Inclusive signs \(\le\) and \(\ge\) include it.

3. Confusing one-variable and two-variable inequalities

One-variable inequalities are shown on a number line. Two-variable inequalities are shown as shaded regions on a coordinate plane.

10Final Summary

  1. An inequality compares two expressions.
  2. Strict inequalities exclude the boundary.
  3. Inclusive inequalities include the boundary.
  4. Reverse the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number.
  5. One-variable inequalities are graphed on number lines.
  6. Two-variable inequalities are graphed as shaded regions.
  7. Quadratic inequalities use roots and intervals.
